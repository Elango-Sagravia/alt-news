style="font-family:Roboto+Serif;font-size:13px;line-height:1;text-align:left;color:#000"><p style="font-size:16px;line-height:1.5">The <a style="text-decoration:none;border-bottom:2px solid #6b705c;color:#000" href="https://link.longandshort.com/ls-15-03-2025-f1-16-kuznets-curve?source=website">Kuznets Curve</a> is an economic theory that shows how income inequality changes as a country develops.</p><p style="font-size:16px;line-height:1.5"><span style="font-size:20px;padding-right:5px">→</span><strong>Early stage (rising inequality):</strong> At the start of development, wealth is style="font-size:20px;padding-right:5px">→</span><strong>Industrialization effect:</strong> As economies shift from farming to industry, urban wages rise, but rural areas still struggle, leading to higher inequality.</p><p style="font-size:16px;line-height:1.5"><span style="font-size:20px;padding-right:5px">→</span><strong>Turning point (peak inequality):</strong> After reaching a certain level of development, countries invest.</p><p style="font-size:16px;line-height:1.5"><span style="font-size:20px;padding-right:5px">→</span><strong>Later Stage (falling inequality):</strong> As the economy grows, workers get better wages, and governments provide welfare programs, reducing inequality.</p><p style="font-size:16px;line-height:1.5"><span style="font-size:20px;padding-right:5px">→</span><strong>Environmental Kuznets Curve (EKC):</strong> A similar idea applies to pollution. Industrialization first increases pollution, but later, richer societies invest in cleaner technology, reducing environmental damage.</p><p style="font-size:16px;line-height:1.5"><strong>Example:</strong> Economic liberalization in the 1990s led to rapid growth but increasing inequality, as wealth concentrated in cities while rural areas lagged.
